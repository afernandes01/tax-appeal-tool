How Cook County Assesses Your Property

Cook County is the only county in Illinois that classifies property into different types, each assessed at a different percentage of fair market value. Residential properties (Class 2) are assessed at 10% of fair market value. Commercial and industrial properties are assessed at 25%.

The Triennial Reassessment Cycle

Cook County's 38 townships rotate through three triads on a 3-year cycle. A successful appeal during a reassessment year can yield three years of savings.

Triad Reassessment Years Status (2026)
North / Northwest suburbs (13 townships) 2022, 2025, 2028 Last reassessed 2025
South / Southwest suburbs (17 townships) 2023, 2026, 2029 Currently being reassessed
City of Chicago (8 townships) 2024, 2027, 2030 Last reassessed 2024

The Four Levels of Appeal

Level 1 — Cook County Assessor's Office. The first and simplest level. File online within 30 days of your reassessment notice at cookcountyassessor.com. Filing is free and takes about 20 minutes. You can submit up to 6 comparable PINs. Decisions arrive within approximately 60 days.

Level 2 — Board of Review (BOR). The critical level. The BOR is a separate body that sets the final county-level valuation. You do not need to file with the Assessor first. File online at appeals.cookcountyboardofreview.com. The BOR opens each township after the Assessor completes its work. Check dates and deadlines here.

Level 3 — Property Tax Appeal Board (PTAB). A state-level body requiring filing within 30 days of the BOR's written decision. PTAB requires at least 3 comparable properties and cases typically take 2+ years. PTAB reductions roll over for the remainder of the triennial period. Forms are at ptab.illinois.gov.

Level 4 — Circuit Court. Filed within 165 days after the first penalty date of the second tax installment. Requires the higher "clear and convincing evidence" standard. The full tax bill must be paid before filing.

Three Bases for Appeal

Lack of Uniformity (most common and typically strongest): Your property is assessed higher per square foot than comparable properties in the same neighborhood and classification. This tool's Uniformity Analysis helps you build this argument.

Overvaluation: The assessed value implies a fair market value that exceeds what comparable homes have recently sold for, or exceeds your own recent purchase price. This tool's Overvaluation Analysis helps you build this argument.

Incorrect Property Description: Factual errors in recorded characteristics like square footage, room count, or property class. Review your property details on the Assessor's website and file a correction if needed.

Why This Matters

Research from the University of Chicago and investigative reporting by ProPublica have shown that Cook County's property tax system is deeply regressive. Lower-value homes are systematically overassessed relative to higher-value homes, shifting the tax burden onto less affluent homeowners.

There are significant racial disparities in who appeals: studies show that appeal rates differ dramatically across neighborhoods, with communities that would benefit most from appeals being least likely to file. Commercial appeal firms charge 25 to 50% of first-year savings for work that is fundamentally data retrieval and analysis.

This tool aims to make that analysis accessible to everyone for free.
